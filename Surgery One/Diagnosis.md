## Diagnosis

This is a pelvic ring fracture characteristic of high-energy trauma. The sacrum is fractured, severing the continuity between the pelvic ring and the spine, and the sacrum has displaced. Because the upper and lower body are connected at this point, attempting to move the body causes the fracture site to shift, with the left half displacing proximally — resulting in severe pain.

The sacral region in particular is comminuted (shattered), making this an extremely serious condition. There is suspicion that nerves have been trapped by the displaced bone fragments, and neurological damage may have already occurred. Specifically, nerves other than those at the right S1 foramen have been injured, and there is a possibility of bladder and rectal dysfunction resulting from nerve damage in that region.

---

## Treatment Options

### Option 1: Conservative Management

Wait for the instability to resolve on its own. This would require approximately **6–8 weeks** of strict bed rest.

**Advantages:** Minimally invasive.

**Disadvantages:**

- Prolonged bed rest with severe pain; even turning the body causes intense pain
- An estimated **2–3 months** of waiting before any meaningful rehabilitation can begin
- Difficulty initiating rehab, with expected decline in walking ability
- Neurological symptoms may not improve, and may worsen
- Higher likelihood of lasting sequelae: pseudoarthrosis (non-union), persistent severe pain, significant leg-length discrepancy, and other complications that may ultimately require surgery anyway

### Option 2: Surgical Fixation (Recommended)

Reconstruct the continuity between the spine and pelvic ring by connecting the **5th lumbar vertebra** to the bilateral pelvic bones using **screws and rods**. If possible, the sacral distal displacement will be reduced and fixed with a **plate** — this will be determined based on the condition of the soft tissues around the buttocks.

- Stabilises the fracture and provides pain relief
- Stabilisation is also expected to allow neurological recovery
- Performed in the **prone position**, approximately **3–4 hours** duration

**Advantages:**

- Fracture fixation reduces pain
- Enables early rehabilitation
- Reduces the likelihood and severity of long-term sequelae
- Creates favourable conditions for bone healing

**Disadvantages:**

- Due to the severe comminution, the anteriorly displaced sacral vertebral body will likely be **difficult to reduce and fixate**
- Potential for surgical complications (see below)

## Surgical Complications

### Infection
Even with prophylactic antibiotics, the risk cannot be eliminated entirely. The infection rate is estimated at around **1–3%**. While the likelihood of infection spreading deep into the bone is even lower, should it occur it could necessitate removal of all metal implants, result in an extremely prolonged treatment period, or in the worst-case scenario, lead to bone resorption and severe long-term complications.

### Soft Tissue Necrosis
Since the buttocks are likely to have suffered a strong impact at the time of injury, there is a risk of **skin necrosis and bone exposure**. Additionally, poor wound healing or partial necrosis may lead to **wound dehiscence** (wound separation). If left untreated, the risk of infection increases, so additional surgery such as a **skin flap procedure** would be performed.

### Bleeding
If the large blood vessels around the pelvis are damaged, **massive bleeding** may occur requiring emergency intervention.

### Nerve Injury
Large nerves run around the pelvis. Surgical manipulation or screw placement may compress nerves, causing **numbness or muscle weakness**. Recovery is expected, but permanent sequelae may remain.

### Re-fracture, Re-displacement, or Hardware Failure
If excessive force is applied before the bone has fully healed, re-fracture or hardware failure may occur, necessitating **reoperation**.

### Hardware Malposition
Despite careful placement, screws may protrude near the skin surface, enter nerve pathways, or penetrate into a joint — which may only be discovered on follow-up imaging. **Reoperation** would be required in such cases.

### Anaesthesia
*(Standard anaesthesia risks apply.)*

### Scarring
Scars will remain. Reduced sensation (numbness) around the wound area may persist.

### Other
Unforeseen complications may occur.

### Implant Removal
Once bone healing is achieved, implant removal will be considered.

## Complications Regardless of Whether Surgery Is Performed

### Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
Due to the injury and the prolonged period of immobility, there is already an elevated risk of developing DVT/PE. Pulmonary embolism carries a risk of **sudden death**.

### Pseudoarthrosis (Non-Union)
This is a highly load-bearing region, and there is a possibility that the bone may fail to unite. The likelihood of non-union is expected to be **lower with surgery**.

### Long-Term Sequelae
Possible lasting effects include: persistent pain, reduced range of motion, muscle weakness, leg-length discrepancy, decreased walking ability, and neurological symptoms. The **severity of sequelae is expected to be less with surgery**.

---

## Clinical Judgement

**This is a severe pelvic ring fracture and surgery is indicated.**
