# Injuries

Summaries have been provided by Claude Opus 4.6 using DICOM images.

## Pelvis / Spine

_The base of the spine (the triangular bone at the very bottom, just above the tailbone) has been shattered into multiple pieces — not a single crack, but broken apart like a smashed tile. This bone is critical because it sits in the centre of the pelvis and acts like the keystone of an arch, holding everything together._

The **sacrum is severely fractured and comminuted** (shattered into multiple pieces rather than a clean break). You can see the fragmentation clearly in the anterior and cranial views — the central posterior “keystone” of the ring is broken apart. There also appears to be sacroiliac joint disruption on the left side, meaning the connection between the sacrum and the left hip bone has been torn apart. On the left anterior side, the pubic rami appear fractured as well — you can see the discontinuity in the inferior ramus area. The left obturator foramen (the hole in the lower pelvis) looks distorted compared to the right.

In classification terms, this pattern is consistent with a **Tile C pelvic ring injury** — meaning the ring is both rotationally and vertically unstable. The entire left hemipelvis appears to have been displaced relative to the right and the spine. This is a high-energy injury pattern (typically MVA, fall from height, or significant crush).

## Elbow

_The elbow is broken in multiple places and partially dislocated. Three key bones that form the elbow joint are all damaged — the tip of the ulna (the pointy elbow bone), the top of the radius (the smaller forearm bone), and a critical stabilising piece at the front of the joint. Small bone chips are floating around the joint space. The elbow is unstable because the bony architecture that keeps it located has been disrupted._

This appears to be a complex **elbow fracture-dislocation**, most consistent with a “terrible triad” pattern or possibly a transolecranon fracture-dislocation. Across the multiple views:

**Coronoid process fracture** — a fragment is clearly separated from the proximal ulna anteriorly (visible in images 1, 4, 5, 6, 8). Looks like at least a Regan-Morrey Type II given the fragment size.

**Radial head fracture** — the radial head shows irregularity and comminution, likely a Mason Type II–III. Several views show articular surface disruption.

**Olecranon involvement** — there’s comminution at the proximal ulna with displaced bony fragments (the small spike-like fragments visible inferiorly in several views).

**Joint subluxation/instability** — multiple views (especially 10–13, the wider shots) show the ulnohumeral relationship is not congruent, with posterior subluxation evident.

## Ribs

_Several ribs are broken along the back, close to where they connect to the spine. You can see the breaks as gaps or misalignments in the rib bones on the 3D model. The spine itself looks okay from these images, but the doctors would check the regular scan slices to be sure. This kind of injury is painful and affects breathing — it typically takes 6–8 weeks to heal, with the main risks being pneumonia from shallow breathing and, depending on severity, potential injury to underlying organs (lung, spleen, kidney depending on the level)._

The demonstrated **multiple posterior rib fractures** — at least 3–4 ribs appear involved, roughly in the T7–T11 region. The fracture lines are visible as cortical discontinuities along the posterior arcs of the ribs, near or at the costovertebral/costotransverse articulations. Some show mild displacement and angulation at the fracture sites. The thoracic vertebral bodies and spinous processes appear grossly intact on the 3D reconstruction, though axial slices would be needed to rule out subtle vertebral body or transverse process fractures. No obvious flail segment from these views, but depending on whether there are anterior fractures as well, that would need to be assessed.


## Wrist

_The wrist is broken in two places. The larger forearm bone (radius) is fractured right at the wrist joint, and the break extends into the joint surface itself — that’s the more serious part because it affects how smoothly the joint moves. The smaller forearm bone (ulna) has a piece snapped off its tip at the wrist side. There’s also a concern that the soft tissue “disc” that cushions and stabilises the two forearm bones where they meet at the wrist may be torn. This type of fracture pattern typically needs surgical fixation (plate and screws, often volar locking plate) to restore the joint surface accurately and prevent long-term arthritis._

**Distal radius fracture** — appears intra-articular with comminution extending to the radiocarpal joint surface. The dorsal view (image 1) shows irregularity and step-off at the articular surface, suggesting a displaced articular fragment. This pattern is consistent with an AO/OTA type B or C distal radius fracture (partial or complete articular involvement).

**Ulnar styloid fracture** — clearly visible in both views, with the styloid process separated from the distal ulna. The lateral view (image 2) shows this more prominently. An ulnar styloid base fracture in particular raises concern for TFCC (triangular fibrocartilage complex) disruption and DRUJ (distal radioulnar joint) instability.

**Carpal alignment** — the dorsal view shows some irregularity in carpal bone spacing, though the 3D rendering makes it difficult to definitively assess ligamentous injury. Worth correlating with axial slices for scapholunate or lunotriquetral widening.
